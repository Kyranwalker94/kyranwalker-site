Hydroponic Lettuces 🥬

Designing and building an ebb and flow hydroponic growing station in my living room during COVID lockdown, no garden required.

About the project

During the 2021 lockdowns I developed a real itch to grow something. The problem: living in a flat with no garden and no outdoor space. The solution: hydroponics.

Rather than soil-based potted plants on a windowsill, I went a step further and designed an ebb and flow (flood and drain) hydroponic system, a method where nutrient-rich water floods a growing tray on a timer, then drains back into a reservoir, delivering food and oxygen to plant roots in cycles.

Lettuces were the obvious choice. Fast growing, don't need intense light levels, and there's an immediate practical payoff, you can actually eat the results.

How ebb and flow works

Ebb and flow (also called flood and drain) is one of the more beginner-accessible hydroponic methods. The basic principle:

  • Plants sit in a growing medium (clay pebbles / hydroton) in a raised tray
  • A submersible pump in the reservoir below triggers on a timer
  • Water floods the tray to a set depth, saturating the roots
  • The pump switches off and water drains back into the reservoir by gravity
  • Cycle repeats, typically 2-4 times per day depending on plant stage
  • Nutrients are added to the reservoir water and pH is monitored and adjusted

The build

The system was designed to fit in the living room without being an eyesore, compact enough to be practical but large enough to grow a meaningful number of plants.

  • Reservoir tank with submersible pump and float valve
  • Flood tray positioned above the reservoir on a frame
  • Clay pebble growing medium with net pot seedling sites
  • Digital timer for pump cycles
  • Full spectrum LED grow light on a separate timer (12/12 or 18/6 cycle)
  • pH meter and nutrient solution management
  • Overflow fitting to cap flood depth and drain automatically

Results

The system worked. Lettuce seedlings went from germination to harvest-ready in around 4-5 weeks, significantly faster than soil growing, which is one of hydroponics' key advantages. The controlled environment meant no slugs, no weather dependence, and consistent results.

The main challenges were keeping pH stable (lettuce prefers 6.0-7.0), ensuring the reservoir didn't run dry between refills, and managing the light schedule so it didn't interfere with the living room in the evenings.
